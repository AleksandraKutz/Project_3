Health Coverage & Pediatric Care Analysis

Summary of Findings:

This study investigates the distribution of pediatricians and the factors that contribute to access to pediatric care in California. Key findings include:

1. High Health Insurance Coverage

California boasts a 96.5% health insurance coverage rate for children, primarily due to programs like Medi-Cal, which provide low-income families with access to affordable healthcare.

2. Pediatrician Shortages

On average, there is one pediatrician for every 1,115 children in California. However, areas like Bakersfield face more severe shortages, primarily due to geographic isolation and a lack of medical professionals.

3. Limited Correlation with Population Density

There is a very weak correlation between population density and the number of children per pediatrician. This suggests that factors other than population density, such as access to healthcare infrastructure, are more influential in shaping pediatric care availability.

4. Family Income and Pediatric Care

While there is a statistically significant relationship between family median income and the children-to-pediatrician ratio, it only explains 5.14% of the variability in pediatric care distribution. This indicates that family income has a relatively minor impact on access to pediatric care.

5. Poverty and Pediatric Care

Similarly, the poverty ratio has a statistically significant relationship with the children-to-pediatrician ratio, but it also accounts for only 5.14% of the variability, highlighting that poverty plays a limited role in shaping the pediatric care landscape.

Conclusion

In conclusion, while socioeconomic factors like family income and poverty are statistically significant, they explain only a small portion of the variability in pediatric care distribution. Geographic factors, such as isolation and access to healthcare infrastructure, appear to play a more critical role in determining access to pediatric services.

Why is this important?

Comparing these factors can help identify areas with limited access to pediatric care and highlight the social factors (such as income, poverty, population density, and insurance coverage) that most affect children's health and access to healthcare. This allows for better planning of health policies and investments in pediatric care in regions that need it the most.
